# SUBLET — New Features

Three new tools built to bring order to the most chaotic parts of a hackathon: the speaker stage, the judging floor, and the final demos.

---

## 1. Live Speaker Stage

**The old way**

Every hackathon has a printed schedule taped to a wall. It says "Speaker A at 2:00 PM." But Speaker A is stuck in traffic. Speaker B finishes ten minutes early. Someone speaks for forty minutes when they had fifteen. By the third talk, the whole schedule is fiction — and no one knows it except the organiser quietly panicking at the back.

**What we built**

A live running order that the organiser controls in real time from any screen. Every speaker gets a countdown timer. When someone runs long, you can see it. When someone is a no-show, you tap one button and they move to the end of the list — the rest of the schedule closes the gap automatically.

If the whole event starts late, you just press Start when you're ready and every time in the schedule adjusts to the real clock, not the printed one.

And because every hackathon deserves a proper host: a **Voice MC** announces each speaker by name when their slot begins, so the room knows who is up without anyone shouting into a microphone.

---

## 2. Group Judging System

**The old way**

Judges walk around a room full of tables. Some projects are popular — a crowd forms, judges wait, the team demos the same thing six times. Other projects sit quietly in the corner and get seen by one person. Some judges arrive late and miss half the room. There is no record of who judged what, and at the end someone has to figure out whether every project was actually seen by enough people. It is loud, slow, and unfair.

**What we built**

Every judge is placed into a group before judging begins. Every project is assigned to a group. Each group of judges goes to each group of projects — once, in order, on a timer. Every project is seen by the same number of judges. Every judge sees the same number of projects. No crowding, no forgotten tables.

If a judge arrives late, the system does not break. The groups that have already moved on continue; the late judge catches up to their group or picks up where they left off. The schedule does not depend on everyone being on time.

Projects also have a submission deadline enforced by the platform, so judges never arrive at a table with nothing to review.

The result: every project gets a fair and equal review, every judge spends their time efficiently, and the organiser can see the whole floor at a glance.

---

## 3. Demo Day Stage

**The old way**

The same paper-schedule problem as the speaker stage — but higher stakes, because this is the final showcase. Teams present to a full room. The MC announces each team from a list. Someone calls the wrong name. The order shifts. A team misses their slot. It feels less like a finale and more like organised confusion.

**What we built**

Everything from the Live Speaker Stage, applied to the final demos: a live running order, per-team countdown timers, one-tap reschedule or skip, and a real-time clock that anchors the schedule to whenever the event actually started.

The **Voice MC** announces each team as they come on stage — project name, what they built — and thanks them when their time is up, so the transition between demos is smooth and the room always knows what is happening next.

The organiser controls the whole show from one screen. The audience just sees a confident, well-run event.
